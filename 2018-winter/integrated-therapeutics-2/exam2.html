1.3 fluid requirements

1.3.1 maintenance

  • adults: 30-40 mL/kg
  • pediatrics: “4, 2, 1” rule
    • 4 mL/kg/hr + 2 mL/kg/hr * (weight > 10kg) + 1 mL/kg/hr * max(0, weight - 20 kg)
    • first 10 kg: 4 mL/kg/hr
    • second 10 kg: 2 mL/kg/hr
    • thereafter: 1 mL/kg/hr

2 shock

2.1 exam   ignore

2.2 definition

acute state characterized by inadequate perfusion of critical organs; can produce serious consequences including death SBP ≤90 mm Hg + end organ damage

2.2.1 types of shock

  • cardiogenic
  • hypovolemic
  • anaphylactic
  • septic
  • neurogenic

2.2.2 stages of shock

  • initial
  • compensatory (warm)
  • progressive (cold)
  • refractory

2.3 causes

  • hypovolemia
  • cardiogenic
  • distributive

2.4 end organ damage   l

2.4.1 brain

2.4.1.1 labs
  • abnormal CT scan
  • abnormal MRI
  • abnormal EEG

2.4.2 heart

2.4.2.1 labs
  • elevated troponins (reference: <0.6 ng/mL)
  • reduced ejection fraction on ECHO (<45%)
  • elevated BNP (reference: 0-99 pg/mL)
  • abnormal EKG—STEMI, NSTEMI

2.4.3 kidneys

2.4.3.1 labs
  • elevated SCr
  • elevated BUN

2.4.4 liver

2.4.4.1 labs
  • elevated LFTs
  • elevated INR

2.5 hemodynamic parameters

2.5.1 CO

  • volume of blood pumped by the ventricle in 1 minute
  • CO = SV × HR

2.5.2 Systemic Venous Oxygen Saturation (SVO2)

measure of tissue oxygenation

2.5.3 Central Venous Pressure (CVP)

correlates to the amount of blood returning to the heart

2.5.4 PCWP

preload

2.5.5 SVR

afterload

2.5.6 by shock etiology

  CO CVP SVO2
hypovolemia down down down
cardiogenic down up down
distributive slightly up down slightly up

  • peripheral and pulmonary edema

  • for dextose: hyperglycemia
  • for LR/Plasmalyte: electrolyte abnormalitiees
2.6.3.2 colloids
  • cardiac failure
  • edema
  • for HES: mortality or renal failure
  • anaphylaxis
  • bleeding
